\abstract{Machine-learning models are now widely used to infer lithology from well logs, yet
the logs encountered in the field rarely behave like the inputs these models assume. Entire
curves go unrecorded for reasons that depend on the logging tool, the operator, and the
acquisition era rather than on chance; the curves that are present differ in character from
one well to the next; a few common rock types account for most of the labels while the rest
appear only rarely; the labels grow ambiguous wherever two beds meet; and the cost of a
mistake depends on how far apart the confused lithologies sit geologically. A model assessed
by splitting depth samples at random can therefore look accurate and still break down on a
well it has not seen, a reduced set of curves, an uncommon lithology, or a bed boundary. We
present GeoMERIT, a robust method for lithology prediction that, instead of relying on a
single stronger classifier, confronts each of these failure modes at the point in the
pipeline where it arises. Missing and anomalous responses are written into a learnable
geological state---together with the identity of the available curve suite, a within-well
normalization, and the behavior of each curve over depth---rather than filled in. Rare
classes, boundary samples, and confidently labeled depths are then allowed to reshape the
training objective. The class probabilities are reorganized by a three-backend
gradient-boosted ensemble, a coarse-to-fine geological hierarchy, and dedicated rare-class
experts, so that uncommon and neighboring lithologies are not overwhelmed by a single flat
softmax. A label is committed only at the final step, chosen to minimize the expected
geological cost under the FORCE penalty matrix rather than to maximize posterior probability.
On the FORCE 2020 dataset (118 wells, 12 lithology classes, 1{,}431{,}383 valid labeled
samples), evaluated by strict 10-fold well-grouped cross-validation that withholds entire
wells, GeoMERIT reaches Weighted F1 $=0.7526$, Boundary F1 $=0.4567$, and Penalty $=-0.5886$,
improving on the published RFE+XGBoost reference by $+3.5\%$, $+11.4\%$, and $+6.4\%$ at once,
with a mean F1 of $0.5061$ over the rarest classes. Because these three measures capture
different aspects of robustness---overall accuracy, stability where beds meet, and the
geological severity of the errors that remain---their simultaneous improvement indicates that
matching each stage of the predictor to the way the data are generated is a more effective
route to robustness than adopting a larger model.}

\keyword{robust lithology prediction; well logs; missing data; long-tailed learning;
boundary samples; cost-sensitive decision; gradient-boosted decision trees; FORCE 2020}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Lithology prediction underpins reservoir characterization, geological modeling, and the
evaluation of hydrocarbon developments~\cite{rider2011,ellis2007,doveton2014}. A well log
measures how a formation responds to a suite of physical probes---gamma ray (GR), bulk density
(RHOB), neutron porosity (NPHI), photoelectric factor (PEF), sonic transit time (DTC),
resistivity (RDEP), and others---recorded continuously with depth, and supervised learning is
now a routine way to turn these measurements into lithology labels at
scale~\cite{hall2016facies,merembayev2021,dev2019formation,carvalho2026benchmark}. Most of
this work, however, carries over the default assumptions of supervised classification: that
inputs are complete, that classes are reasonably balanced, that training and test samples come
from the same distribution, and that every error costs the same. Logs acquired in the field
honor none of these assumptions.

In well logs these departures are not incidental but structural. The missing values do not
fall at random: whole curves are absent across entire wells because tool strings, service
companies, and acquisition vintages vary, so the pattern of what is missing is itself a clue
to the data. The values that are recorded drift between wells through calibration and borehole
conditions, and are further perturbed by tool malfunctions and noise. The label distribution
is steeply long-tailed, with a handful of lithologies dominating and the rest barely
represented. The labels are least reliable at bed boundaries, where one rock type grades into
the next. And the penalty for a mistake is uneven---confusing two similar rocks matters
little, whereas mistaking a rock for a geologically distant one can be costly. The public
FORCE 2020 benchmark~\cite{bormann2020force} exhibits all of these properties at once. Across
its 1{,}431{,}383 valid labeled samples from 118 Norwegian North Sea wells, Shale alone
accounts for 61.3\% of the labels while Basement contributes just 141 rows (0.01\%)---a
head-to-tail ratio near $6220{:}1$---and this imbalance coexists with whole log families
absent from particular wells (Figure~\ref{fig:missingness_matrix}). The benchmark also
supplies a $12{\times}12$ penalty matrix that grades geological severity: predicting
Sandstone/Shale for true Sandstone counts as a minor error, whereas predicting Halite for the
same rock counts as a serious one. The practical consequence is not just that accuracy falls
short; prediction becomes \emph{unstable}. A model can appear excellent when depth samples are
shuffled into random folds---which lets well-specific acquisition signatures and local
stratigraphic continuity leak across the train/test divide~\cite{rosenblatt2024,roberts2017cv,mila2022nndm}---and
then fail once it meets an unseen well, a depleted curve suite, a rare lithology, or a boundary
interval.

Each of these difficulties has been studied, but almost always one at a time, and a remedy
aimed at one tends to leave the others untouched. The usual response to missing curves is to
reconstruct them, whether by statistical imputation or by generative
models~\cite{vanbuuren2011mice,stekhoven2012missforest,liu2025wellgan,xie2024cgan}; doing so,
however, overwrites the missingness pattern, which in well logs is itself informative, since
the choice to run a curve reflects the vintage, the operator, the borehole condition, and the
target formation, all correlated with geology~\cite{chen2026rfe,little2019stats,josse2024missing}.
Methods for class imbalance reweight the loss or synthesize minority
examples~\cite{chawla2002smote,lin2017focal,cui2019classbalanced,he2024imbalanced}, but they
say nothing about the bed boundaries where most of the stratigraphic interest lies. Deep
sequence and image models extract powerful representations, yet they generally need complete
curve suites and large labeled corpora and, once wells are held out in evaluation, have not
reliably surpassed gradient-boosted
trees~\cite{wang2024dnnlitho,grinsztajn2022trees,shwartzziv2022tabular,mcelfresh2024benchmark}.
And nearly every published system still selects its label by maximizing posterior probability,
which weighs all errors equally even though the benchmark provides a cost matrix that does
not~\cite{elkan2001cost,berger1985decision}. The closest precedent treats data quality as a
feature: a recent robust-feature-engineering (RFE) pipeline with a quality-aware XGBoost
classifier~\cite{chen2026rfe} lifted Weighted F1 from $0.6640$ to $0.7272$ on FORCE 2020 under
well-grouped validation simply by encoding missingness instead of repairing it. The question
we take up begins where that result ends: \emph{how much more robust can prediction become when
data quality, the long tail, boundary uncertainty, and geological cost are handled
together---in the representation, the training objective, the posterior, and the decision
rule---rather than in the features alone?}

We address this question with GeoMERIT (Missingness-encoding, Ensembling, Robust-features,
Imbalance-aware, Tail-correction), a method built for logs that are incomplete, heterogeneous
across wells, and long-tailed. Its premise is that robustness is not the property of any one
strong classifier but the cumulative effect of handling each data pathology where it first
enters the pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:method}, Figure~\ref{fig:framework},
Table~\ref{tab:layers}). The pipeline begins by turning the raw evidence---which curves are
absent, which responses are anomalous, which suite of curves a well carries, how each response
looks once normalized within its own well, and how it varies locally with depth---into an
explicit state the model can read, so that data quality informs the prediction instead of
being smoothed away. On that state, a combined class--boundary--confidence weighting tilts the
objective toward the rare lithologies and the bed transitions a uniform objective would
overlook. The probabilities are then restructured by a three-backend tree ensemble, a
coarse-to-fine geological hierarchy, and dedicated tail experts, each resolving common,
neighboring, and rare lithologies at the granularity it needs, so that the dominant classes do
not absorb rare-class probability. Only then is a label chosen, and it is chosen to minimize
expected geological cost under the FORCE penalty matrix rather than to maximize posterior
probability. Throughout, we treat well-grouped validation as the test of robustness, not as
part of the method: holding out whole wells imitates the real task of predicting into a well
the model has never logged, and the aim is to stay stable under whatever missingness,
heterogeneity, imbalance, and cost asymmetry that new well brings. Under this protocol
GeoMERIT improves on the RFE+XGBoost reference at the same time on Weighted F1
($0.7272\!\to\!0.7526$, $+3.5\%$), Boundary F1 ($0.4100\!\to\!0.4567$, $+11.4\%$), and FORCE
Penalty ($-0.6289\!\to\!-0.5886$, $+6.4\%$), and lifts the mean F1 over the rarest classes to
$0.5061$.

The contribution is therefore a single framework that treats missingness, cross-well
heterogeneity, long-tailed supervision, boundary ambiguity, and asymmetric cost not as
nuisances to remove before learning but as structure to model during it. It rests on four
design choices, each addressing one of these conditions and each testable on its own.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A missingness-aware geological state representation.} Instead of imputing absent
curves---which would assume the missingness is ignorable---we fold curve absence, anomalous
responses, curve-suite identity, within-well normalization, local depth context, and
stratigraphic metadata into one state vector, letting the model learn which curves are missing,
in which combinations, and how that relates to lithology. The testable prediction is that
accuracy degrades gracefully, rather than collapsing, on wells with incomplete suites
(Sections~\ref{sec:repr} and \ref{sec:rq3}).
\item \textbf{A learning rule that protects the tail and the boundaries.} Each sample is scaled
by a product of three factors---an effective-number class weight, an up-weighting of boundary
depths, and a discount for low-confidence labels---which moves the objective off the dominant
classes and onto rare lithologies and bed transitions. This raises the mean tail F1 to
$0.5061$, roughly $1.6\times$ the single-model level, and drives the largest relative gain
among the primary metrics, $+11.4\%$ on Boundary F1 (Sections~\ref{sec:learning} and
\ref{sec:rq4}).
\item \textbf{A structured posterior over lithologies.} In place of one flat 12-way softmax,
GeoMERIT combines the LightGBM, XGBoost, and CatBoost posteriors as a geometric mean, blends in
a coarse-to-fine hierarchy over six geological super-classes, and gates six one-vs-rest experts
for the rare classes, so that the dominant classes no longer absorb rare-class probability and
adjacent lithologies are separated by experts trained for that purpose
(Sections~\ref{sec:posterior} and \ref{sec:rq2}).
\item \textbf{A decision layer keyed to geological cost.} The final choice is reframed from
picking the most probable class to taking the action of least geological risk: logit adjustment
first removes the long-tailed prior, and Bayes-risk decoding against the official FORCE penalty
matrix then returns the label of lowest expected cost. To our knowledge this is the first
FORCE 2020 system whose reported predictions are produced this way, and it accounts for the
$+6.4\%$ improvement in Penalty (Sections~\ref{sec:decision} and \ref{sec:rq5}).
\end{enumerate}

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} places GeoMERIT among
four families of prior work. Section~\ref{sec:data} introduces the dataset, the evaluation
metrics, and the well-grouped protocol. Section~\ref{sec:method} formalizes the four layers.
Section~\ref{sec:setup} gives the experimental setup and the basis for fair comparison.
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the results, from overall held-out-well performance, through
the origin of the gains, to behavior on feature-deficient wells, rare lithologies, and the
decision layer. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} examines failure modes and limitations, and
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
We group prior work into four families, each defined by the aspect of real-data complexity
it addresses, and locate GeoMERIT by what each family leaves open.

\subsection{Well-Log Lithology Classification and Benchmarking}
Machine learning lithology classification has progressed from early facies
challenges~\cite{hall2016facies} through systematic algorithm
comparisons~\cite{merembayev2021,dev2019formation} to deep architectures including CNNs,
recurrent models, transformers, diffusion-based augmentation, and multi-scale hierarchical
networks~\cite{wang2024dnnlitho,chen2025geological,zhao2025diffusion,zhan2025mchgeo}.
Interpretability-oriented hybrids such as SHAP-based meta-learning
transformers~\cite{sun2024shapmeta,lundberg2017shap} and refined-lithology
pipelines~\cite{shi2023refined} demonstrate strong within-dataset accuracy. Benchmark studies
make the comparability problem explicit. Published scores are frequently incomparable because
splits, label sets, and metrics differ, and reported performance can swing by 10--20 absolute
F1 points with the validation protocol alone, because the unit of geological generalization
is the well, not the row~\cite{carvalho2026benchmark,rosenblatt2024}. What remains open is a
method \emph{designed}---not merely evaluated---for stability under the data conditions that
cause those swings. GeoMERIT adopts the benchmark literature's protocol discipline and builds
the corresponding robustness mechanisms into every stage of the model.

\subsection{Missingness and Data-Quality Modeling in Well Logs}
The dominant response to incomplete logs is reconstruction, most commonly through chained-equation
and forest imputation~\cite{vanbuuren2011mice,stekhoven2012missforest} or through generative
imputation with sequence GANs and conditional GANs~\cite{liu2025wellgan,xie2024cgan}. Reconstruction is
appropriate when missingness is ignorable. Well-log missingness, however, is structurally
non-random because whole curves are absent as a result of tool-string choices that correlate
with vintage, operator, and target formation~\cite{chen2026rfe}. Statistical learning theory shows
that supervised learners can remain consistent when missingness is encoded as input rather
than repaired~\cite{little2019stats,josse2024missing}, and the RFE
study~\cite{chen2026rfe} demonstrated the practical value of this position on FORCE 2020.
The open question is how far the ``missingness as signal'' paradigm extends beyond features
into training and decision-making. GeoMERIT extends it through representation, weighting,
posterior structure, and decoding.

\subsection{Long-Tailed and Boundary-Aware Learning}
General-purpose remedies for class imbalance include synthetic
oversampling~\cite{chawla2002smote}, focal loss~\cite{lin2017focal}, margin-based
losses~\cite{cao2019ldam}, effective-number class weighting~\cite{cui2019classbalanced}, and
prior-corrected logit adjustment~\cite{menon2021logit}; surveys document their
trade-offs~\cite{he2024imbalanced}. Two domain constraints restrict their transfer to well
logs. First, synthetic interpolation of log rows can violate depth continuity and
stratigraphic consistency, generating physically implausible samples; we therefore avoid
synthetic oversampling entirely. Second, lithology boundaries concentrate both label
ambiguity and practical interest, so imbalance handling must not sacrifice boundary
fidelity---a requirement most imbalance methods never examine. GeoMERIT combines
effective-number weighting~\cite{cui2019classbalanced} with explicit boundary up-weighting and
treats logit adjustment~\cite{menon2021logit} as a prior-shift correction applied at decision
time, where its temperature can be tuned against the geological cost directly.

\subsection{Cost-Sensitive and Geology-Aware Decision Making}
Cost-sensitive learning theory establishes that when misclassification costs are asymmetric,
the optimal decision minimizes expected cost rather than maximizing posterior
probability~\cite{elkan2001cost,berger1985decision,andrade2019bayes,zadrozny2002multiclass}.
FORCE 2020 operationalizes geological cost in its penalty matrix, yet most published
systems---including the RFE reference~\cite{chen2026rfe}---still decode by argmax and report
the penalty only as an evaluation metric. Sequence decoders such as Viterbi smoothing and
(semi-)Markov CRFs~\cite{viterbi1967,lafferty2001crf,sarawagi2004semimarkov} optimize label
continuity but not geological cost. To our knowledge, GeoMERIT is the first FORCE 2020 system
whose reported predictions are produced by Bayes-risk decoding over the official penalty
matrix, coupled with logit adjustment in a single, jointly tuned decision layer.

\section{Dataset and Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}
We use the public FORCE 2020 lithofacies dataset~\cite{bormann2020force}, which contains
$1{,}431{,}383$ valid labeled samples from 118 wells and 12 lithology classes. Each sample is
indexed by measured depth and may contain up to 20 well-log curves, including GR, SGR, RHOB,
DRHO, NPHI, PEF, DTC, DTS, RDEP, RSHA, RMED, RXO, RMIC, SP, CALI, BS, DCAL, ROP, ROPA, and
MUDWEIGHT. We also use well-head coordinates and NPD stratigraphic tables, including GROUP,
FORMATION, and casing-depth information. These variables define a realistic lithology
prediction setting in which the model must infer rock type from heterogeneous curve suites,
uneven sample support, and geological labels whose errors have unequal consequences.

The first source of heterogeneity is spatial and operational. The 118 wells are distributed
across multiple North Sea blocks, and the available curve suites differ across those blocks.
Figure~\ref{fig:well_locations} therefore serves as the entry point to the dataset. It shows
where the wells are located, how many labeled samples each well contributes, and how much of
its log suite is missing on average.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/fig01_well_locations_v3.pdf}
\ShortCenteredFigCaption{Spatial distribution of FORCE 2020 wells.}{fig:well_locations}
\end{figure}

In Figure~\ref{fig:well_locations}, marker color represents the per-well mean missing rate,
and marker size represents the number of labeled samples in each well. The figure shows that
data quality is not uniformly distributed across the basin. Nearby wells can share similar
missingness patterns, while different blocks exhibit different acquisition regimes. This
means that a held-out well is not merely another independent row sample; it may correspond to
a new logging regime with a different combination of available and unavailable curves.

The second difficulty is the supervision structure. Even when curves are present, the labels
are highly imbalanced, so a model trained only to maximize aggregate accuracy can be dominated
by the most frequent lithologies. Figure~\ref{fig:lithology_longtail} isolates this label-side
problem before we examine the input-side missingness in more detail.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{figures/fig02_lithology_distribution_line.pdf}
\ShortCenteredFigCaption{Long-tailed lithology class distribution.}{fig:lithology_longtail}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:lithology_longtail} shows a long-tailed distribution spanning roughly four
orders of magnitude. Shale contains $877{,}043$ samples, accounting for 61.3\% of all valid
labels, whereas Basement contains only 141 samples, or about 0.01\%. The resulting
head-to-tail ratio is approximately $6220{:}1$, and the distribution has a Gini coefficient
of about 0.77. This is why a classifier trained for aggregate accuracy tends to fit the
abundant head classes well while under-learning the rare lithologies and the thin
stratigraphic events.

The spatial heterogeneity in Figure~\ref{fig:well_locations} and the label imbalance in
Figure~\ref{fig:lithology_longtail} interact with a third property. The log curves are not
missing independently. Figure~\ref{fig:missingness_matrix} makes this input-side structure
explicit by arranging wells and curves as a missing-fraction matrix.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.86\linewidth]{figures/fig03_missingness_vAB_merged.pdf}
\ShortCenteredFigCaption{Well-by-curve missing-fraction matrix.}{fig:missingness_matrix}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:missingness_matrix} groups the curves into the petrophysical families Gamma,
Density/Neutron, Sonic, Resistivity, SP, Caliper, and Drilling/mud. Wells are sorted by their
mean missing rate, while the marginal bars summarize per-curve and per-well missingness. The
block patterns show that missingness is structured by both well and curve family. Entire log
families, such as Sonic or Resistivity, can be absent in specific wells, whereas other curve
families remain available. This is the key reason we do not treat missing values as random
noise to be imputed away. In this dataset, missingness carries information about tool-string
selection, acquisition vintage, and operational conditions, all of which can correlate with
geology. The representation layer in Section~\ref{sec:repr} is therefore designed to encode
missingness and curve-suite identity directly rather than erase them during preprocessing.

Finally, robust lithology prediction also requires a geology-aware evaluation target. The
FORCE 2020 benchmark provides an official $12{\times}12$ penalty matrix, shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:penalty}. This matrix assigns small costs to sedimentologically adjacent
confusions, such as Sandstone versus Sandstone/Shale, and larger costs to distant confusions,
such as Sandstone versus Halite.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{figures/fig2_penalty_matrix.pdf}
\ShortCenteredFigCaption{Geological penalty matrix.}{fig:penalty}
\end{figure}

The penalty matrix in Figure~\ref{fig:penalty} makes the evaluation problem different from
ordinary multi-class classification. Entry $A_{ij}$ is the cost of predicting class $j$ when
the true class is $i$; the diagonal cost is zero, within-environment confusions are relatively
cheap, and cross-environment confusions can cost up to 4. Consequently, posterior argmax is
not necessarily the best geological decision. GeoMERIT uses this matrix at the decision layer
by minimizing expected geological penalty rather than treating all errors as equal.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics: Three Facets of Robustness}
\label{sec:metrics}
We report five complementary metrics. Weighted F1 measures aggregate classification quality,
whereas Macro F1 gives the unweighted mean over the 12 classes. Boundary F1 is computed only
on samples within one depth step of a lithology change and therefore measures stratigraphic
transition quality. Penalty is the mean of $-A_{y\hat y}$ under the FORCE matrix, with values
closer to zero indicating less severe geological errors. Tail mean F1 is the unweighted mean
F1 over the six rarest classes, namely Tuff, Halite, Coal, Dolomite, Anhydrite, and Basement. The three primary metrics probe three distinct facets
of robust prediction and are not interchangeable. Weighted F1 reflects overall prediction
quality and can rise simply by getting more Shale rows right. Boundary F1 reflects stability
in formation-transition zones and rises only by resolving transitions. Penalty reflects the
geological severity of the errors that remain and improves only by avoiding geologically
distant confusions. A method that improves one facet by sacrificing another is not robust;
we therefore evaluate all three jointly.

\subsection{Held-Out-Well Validation as a Robustness Test}
\label{sec:protocol}
We evaluate exclusively under 10-fold GroupKFold cross-validation with well identity as the
grouping key. This protocol is the experimental condition that tests robustness, not part of
the method's definition. GeoMERIT's goal is not to adapt one specific well to another, but to
remain stable under the curve missingness, acquisition heterogeneity, long-tail structure,
and cost asymmetry that any previously unseen well may present, and holding out whole wells
is how that stability is measured. Random row-wise splits place adjacent depth samples from
the same well on both sides of the train/test boundary, allowing models to exploit
well-specific acquisition signatures and local stratigraphic continuity; such splits estimate
interpolation performance within already-logged wells and systematically overstate practical
performance~\cite{rosenblatt2024,roberts2017cv,mila2022nndm}. All feature construction that
uses depth context---window statistics, gradients, boundary masks---is computed strictly
within each well sequence, so no information crosses well boundaries at any stage.

This protocol deliberately exposes a rare-class limitation. For classes concentrated in a few
wells, some training folds contain no positive examples. Basement (141 rows) is the extreme
case. This is not an artifact of the split but a property of the prediction problem itself---a
new well may contain a lithology the training wells never sampled---so we report Basement
separately and discuss open-set treatment as the appropriate future remedy
(Section~\ref{sec:failures}).

\subsection{Statistical Reporting}
\label{sec:stats}
The reported values are pooled out-of-fold (OOF) metrics over all $1{,}431{,}383$ predictions.
Because pooled values hide fold-to-fold variation, we additionally report the mean
$\pm$ standard deviation across the 10 folds and, for each primary metric, a one-sided
one-sample Wilcoxon signed-rank test~\cite{wilcoxon1945} of the 10 fold values against the
published RFE+XGBoost reference value (alternative: GeoMERIT exceeds the reference). This
test is conservative in one respect (the reference is a pooled point estimate, not a matched
per-fold series) and we interpret it accordingly in Section~\ref{sec:rq1}.

\section{GeoMERIT: A Four-Layer Robust Prediction Framework}
\label{sec:method}
The design of GeoMERIT rests on a single principle: each condition of real well-log data that
would destabilize a conventional classifier---\emph{non-random missingness, acquisition
heterogeneity, long-tailed supervision, boundary ambiguity, and asymmetric geological
cost}---is modeled explicitly at the stage of the pipeline where it arises. This principle
gives rise to four layers (Table~\ref{tab:layers}, Figure~\ref{fig:framework}), each
addressing one of these conditions. We first fix notation. Let $x_i$ denote the log-response
vector at depth sample $i$, $w(i)$ its training weight, $p(y\,|\,\phi(x_i))$ the class
posterior over the state representation $\phi(x_i)$, and $A$ the FORCE penalty matrix. The
representation layer constructs a missingness-aware state vector $\phi(x_i)$ that preserves
data-quality and curve-suite information (Section~\ref{sec:repr}). The learning layer then
estimates posteriors under class-, boundary-, and confidence-aware weighting
(Section~\ref{sec:learning}). The posterior layer structures these probabilities with a model
ensemble, a geological hierarchy, and tail experts (Section~\ref{sec:posterior}). Finally, the
decision layer assigns each label as
\begin{equation}
\hat y_i = \arg\min_{j\in\{1,\ldots,12\}} \sum_{k=1}^{12} A_{kj}\;\hat p\!\bigl(y=k\,\big|\,\phi(x_i)\bigr),
\label{eq:bayes}
\end{equation}
after long-tail logit adjustment (Section~\ref{sec:decision}). Equation~(\ref{eq:bayes}) is the
classical Bayes decision rule under an asymmetric loss~\cite{berger1985decision}: it reduces to
argmax when $A$ is the 0--1 loss and departs from argmax precisely where geological costs are
asymmetric. Unlike a conventional feature-engineering pipeline, in which a single model is
optimized for aggregate accuracy, each stage here targets a specific, named data condition.

\subsection{Missingness-Aware Geologic State Representation (Representation Layer)}
\label{sec:repr}
In well-log data, the absence of a curve is rarely random noise; it records how the data were
acquired. Which curves exist depends on the tool string, the vintage, and the borehole
condition, so entire log families are missing across a whole well rather than scattered at
random (Figure~\ref{fig:missingness_matrix}). Conventional preprocessing treats this absence as a
defect to be repaired by mean or regression imputation, but on well logs that repair carries
two costs at once. The imputed values introduce error that is itself well-correlated, and the
acquisition information that the missingness pattern encodes is erased along with the gap.
Because that pattern is precisely what separates one well's regime from another's, we abandon
imputation and instead encode missingness directly as a learnable geological state.

Concretely, for every curve $c$ we detect missing entries (NaN/Inf) and anomalies (within-well
robust $z$-score $|z|>5$) and produce a sentinel-encoded copy $c_\text{enc}$ in which missing
values become $-999$ and outliers $-777$, alongside binary indicators $c_\text{is\_missing}$
and $c_\text{is\_outlier}$; per-row aggregates of the missing count, missing ratio, outlier
count, and outlier ratio summarize the data quality at each depth. The effect is mechanical
but consequential. Because a GBDT routes sentinel values down a dedicated branch at every
split, it can learn rules of the form ``if PEF is absent, decide from GR and NPHI instead''
with no hand-written logic, so the model learns not only from the observed responses but from
which curves are missing and in which combinations.

Within each well $w$ and curve $c$ we add a robust normalization
\begin{equation}
z_{w,c}(i) = \frac{c(i)-\operatorname{median}_w(c)}{\operatorname{MAD}_w(c)+\epsilon},\qquad \epsilon=10^{-9},
\label{eq:zscore}
\end{equation}
with a standard-deviation fallback when $\operatorname{MAD}_w(c)=0$. Both the absolute
encoded value and the well-relative value are retained. The absolute response separates
lithologies with distinctive global signatures (e.g., Halite), while the well-relative offset
compensates for calibration and borehole-environment drift between wells---precisely the
inter-well heterogeneity that destabilizes transfer to a new well.

Local depth context is captured by window statistics and stabilized gradients. For the eight
primary curves (GR, RHOB, NPHI, PEF, DTC, RDEP, RMED, SP) and radii $r\in\{2,5,10\}$ we
compute window mean, standard deviation, minimum, maximum, and endpoint difference
($8\!\times\!3\!\times\!5=120$ features), plus the depth-normalized gradient
\begin{equation}
\nabla c(i) = \frac{c(i)-c(i-1)}{\text{DEPTH}(i)-\text{DEPTH}(i-1)+\epsilon}.
\label{eq:grad}
\end{equation}
All context features are computed strictly within each well. Four diagnostic combinations
from the classical interpretation playbook (density--porosity separation $\text{RHOB}-\text{NPHI}$;
the ratios $\text{DTC}/|\text{RHOB}|$, $\text{GR}/\log(1+\text{RDEP})$, and
$\text{PEF}/|\text{RHOB}|$) inject petrophysical priors for separating carbonates from
clastics~\cite{rider2011,doveton2014}.

Finally, the per-well curve-availability bit string is hashed into a categorical
\texttt{curve\_suite\_id}---a single feature that captures vendor-, era-, and tool-driven
variation---and well-head coordinates, distance to the centroid of all wells, NPD GROUP and
FORMATION labels, and casing-depth differences complete the state vector $\phi(x_i)$. Missing
stratigraphic labels are encoded as the explicit category ``Unknown'' rather than dropped.

\subsection{Long-Tail- and Boundary-Enhanced Risk Weighting (Learning Layer)}
\label{sec:learning}
Under a uniform training objective, the most frequent classes dominate the aggregate loss and
therefore the gradient, leaving little capacity for rare classes and boundary samples. Shale
alone accounts for $61\%$ of the samples, so under equal weighting the optimizer can reduce the
overall loss largely by classifying Shale correctly, while the rare classes and the boundary
samples that carry the greatest stratigraphic value contribute too little to the objective to
be learned well. To counteract this, the learning layer reweights the training samples so that
these under-represented cases gain proportionally greater influence. Each training sample
receives a multiplicative weight
\begin{equation}
w(i) = w_\text{class}\!\bigl(y(i)\bigr)\cdot w_\text{bnd}(i)\cdot w_\text{conf}(i).
\label{eq:weight}
\end{equation}
The class term uses the effective-number-of-samples
formulation~\cite{cui2019classbalanced},
\begin{equation}
w_\text{class}(k) = \min\!\left(\frac{1-\rho}{1-\rho^{n_k}},\; w_\text{max}\right),\qquad \rho=0.9995,\quad w_\text{max}=8.0,
\label{eq:effn}
\end{equation}
where $n_k$ is the class support in the training fold; weights are rescaled to unit mean.
Equation~(\ref{eq:effn}) functions as a saturation-aware inverse frequency. Once a class has
enough samples that new ones are redundant, additional support stops reducing its weight,
while the cap $w_\text{max}$ prevents the rarest classes from destabilizing training.
Boundary samples (those whose label differs from an immediate depth neighbor) receive weight
$1.2$ and interior samples $0.7$, shifting weight toward transitions without neglecting the
common classes. The confidence term maps the FORCE interpreter-confidence field
$1/2/3$ to $1.0/0.8/0.6$, discounting samples the original labelers themselves marked as
uncertain. We deliberately avoid synthetic oversampling~\cite{chawla2002smote} because interpolated
log rows can violate depth continuity and produce physically impossible curve combinations.

\subsection{Structured Posterior Modeling: Ensemble, Hierarchy, and Tail Experts (Posterior Layer)}
\label{sec:posterior}
A single flat 12-class classifier is the standard baseline, but it forces one softmax to
perform three distinct discriminations simultaneously: separating geologically adjacent classes
(Limestone vs.\ Chalk), separating distant ones (Sandstone vs.\ Halite), and detecting a
141-sample class (Basement). Because the head-class priors dominate the softmax normalization,
the posterior mass available to rare classes is compressed toward zero. The posterior layer
retains the flat classifier but augments it with three coordinated mechanisms that restore
resolution where the flat softmax loses it.

The first mechanism replaces the lone classifier with three complementary backbones trained
per fold---LightGBM~\cite{ke2017lightgbm} on the full training partition ($\approx\!1.28$M rows),
XGBoost~\cite{chen2016xgboost} on a 750{,}000-row subsample, and
CatBoost~\cite{prokhorenkova2018catboost} on a 300{,}000-row subsample, both subsamples
preserving all tail rows and CatBoost run with $\texttt{classes\_count}=12$ so no class is
silently dropped from folds lacking it. Their inductive biases differ (leaf-wise growth with
native categorical support; depth-wise regularized histogram splitting; ordered boosting with
target statistics). Their posteriors are fused geometrically in log space as
\begin{equation}
\hat p(y=k\,|\,\phi)\propto\exp\!\left(\sum_m \alpha_m\log p^{(m)}(y=k\,|\,\phi)\right),\qquad
\boldsymbol\alpha=(1.0,\,0.8,\,0.8).
\label{eq:geomean}
\end{equation}
The geometric mean acts as a product of experts in which a class receives high fused probability only
if no backend strongly vetoes it, which is more robust than an arithmetic mean when one
backend is miscalibrated on a rare class.

The second mechanism restructures the label space so that the confusions that remain stay
within geologically similar groups, where they cost little. The 12 classes are mapped to six
super-classes that mirror interpreter taxonomy
(Table~\ref{tab:superclass}); a coarse classifier predicts the super-class, per-group experts
predict the fine class within it, and the hierarchical posterior
\begin{equation}
p_\text{c2f}(y=k\,|\,\phi) = p\!\bigl(\text{super}(k)\,|\,\phi\bigr)\cdot p\!\bigl(k\,|\,\phi,\text{super}(k)\bigr)
\label{eq:c2f}
\end{equation}
is mixed with the flat posterior using weight $\mu=0.20$ (validation grid). The hierarchy
confines easy mistakes inside sedimentologically coherent groups. Confusing Chalk with
Limestone is a within-carbonate error the fine expert can arbitrate with carbonate-specific
evidence, instead of a flat-softmax competition against Shale's prior mass.

The third mechanism adds dedicated rare-class discriminators that work outside the 12-way
competition. For each tail class $k\in\mathcal T=\{\text{Tuff, Halite,
Coal, Dolomite, Anhydrite, Basement}\}$ a one-vs-rest LightGBM expert with $5\times$
up-weighted positives produces a score $s_k$. When the expert is confident, the corresponding
posterior entry is gated upward:
\begin{equation}
\tilde p(y=k\,|\,\phi) = \hat p(y=k\,|\,\phi)\!\left(1+\gamma_k\,\mathbf 1\bigl[s_k>\theta_k\bigr]\right),
\label{eq:gate}
\end{equation}
followed by renormalization. The gate restores posterior mass that the long tail suppressed,
but only where a dedicated binary discriminator---trained without the 12-way
competition---finds strong evidence. A single global pair $(\gamma,\theta)$ serves all tail
classes, with a separate, stricter Dolomite pair $(\gamma_\text{dol},\theta_\text{dol})$
because Dolomite trades off directly against the penalty (Section~\ref{sec:rq5}).

\subsection{Geological-Cost-Guided Decision Layer (Decision Layer)}
\label{sec:decision}
Two problems survive even a well-structured posterior. Decoding it by argmax still treats
every error as equally bad, which the asymmetric FORCE costs flatly contradict, and a
posterior trained on a long-tailed fold still carries the head-class prior into that decode.
The decision layer removes both before any label is committed. It first applies logit
adjustment~\cite{menon2021logit},
\begin{equation}
\hat p_\text{LA}(y=k\,|\,\phi)\propto \tilde p(y=k\,|\,\phi)\cdot \pi_k^{-\tau},
\label{eq:logitadj}
\end{equation}
where $\pi_k$ is the training-fold prior and $\tau\geq 0$ a temperature; this is a post-hoc
correction of the label prior shift between the long-tailed training distribution and the
evaluation objective, not a heuristic boost. The adjusted posterior then enters the
Bayes-risk rule of Equation~(\ref{eq:bayes}), so the final label is the one with the lowest
expected geological cost rather than the highest probability. The full decision layer has
five interpretable parameters---$\tau$, the global gate $(\gamma,\theta)$, and the Dolomite gate
$(\gamma_\text{dol},\theta_\text{dol})$---searched on a small validation grid (192
configurations), yielding $\tau=0.2$, $\gamma=1.2$, $\theta=0.35$,
$\gamma_\text{dol}=8.0$, $\theta_\text{dol}=0.05$. Each parameter has a physical interpretation (e.g.,
$\theta_\text{dol}=0.05$ with $\gamma_\text{dol}=8.0$ means ``trust the dolomite expert only
at high confidence, then trust it strongly''), so domain experts can retune the operating
point without retraining.

An optional Viterbi pass over each well's depth sequence~\cite{viterbi1967,lafferty2001crf,sarawagi2004semimarkov}
can smooth the decoded labels with an empirical transition matrix; it is examined in
ablation but excluded from the reported numbers to keep the decision layer purely pointwise
and tree-only.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Compared Methods and Fairness}
\label{sec:compared}
The primary references are Baseline XGBoost (no feature engineering, no weighting),
RFE+XGBoost, and the RFE+\{Random Forest, CatBoost, LightGBM\} variants, all as published
in~\cite{chen2026rfe} and all evaluated on FORCE 2020 under a comparable well-grouped 10-fold
protocol, which makes them the strongest directly comparable tree baselines available. The
comparison is conservative in two respects. First, the published baselines are taken at the
operating points reported by their authors. Second, GeoMERIT’s decision-layer grid was tuned
only on validation folds under the same GroupKFold protocol used for reporting.

\subsection{Implementation and Scale}
\label{sec:impl}
All experiments ran on a single 112-core CPU machine with 251~GB RAM (no GPU). The software
stack is LightGBM, XGBoost, CatBoost, scikit-learn~\cite{pedregosa2011sklearn}, NumPy,
Pandas, and PyArrow. Each fold trains the three backends, the coarse-to-fine cascade, and
six tail experts; full 10-fold training takes about 3~h and the decoding-parameter search
about 1~h. The feature table occupies $\approx\!12$~GB in Parquet form and peak training
memory is $\approx\!90$~GB.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
We present the results in three stages: overall performance, the origin of the gains, and
behavior on the most difficult cases. We begin under held-out-well validation by asking
whether GeoMERIT improves overall
classification, boundary recovery, and geological cost together, and whether the gains exceed
fold-to-fold noise (Section~\ref{sec:rq1}). We then trace where those gains originate,
separating the contributions of the representation, learning, posterior, and decision layers
(Section~\ref{sec:rq2}). Turning to the conditions the design was built for, we stratify the
test wells by missingness to probe stability where curves are absent (Section~\ref{sec:rq3}),
then break performance down by lithology to see which rare classes and boundary zones remain
unresolved and whether the residual errors are geologically coherent (Section~\ref{sec:rq4}).
Finally, we sweep the decision-layer parameters to ask whether cost-guided prediction is
tunable and dependable enough for field use (Section~\ref{sec:rq5}).

\subsection{Main Comparison Under Held-Out-Well Validation}
\label{sec:rq1}
GeoMERIT is meant to improve three things at once---overall classification quality, stability
across bed boundaries, and the geological severity of its mistakes---by strengthening all four
layers together rather than tuning any one of them in isolation. Because a method can raise one
of these at the expense of another, we report all three under a single held-out-well protocol,
comparing against the published RFE+XGBoost reference on the same 10-fold GroupKFold by well and
reading Weighted F1 as overall quality, Boundary F1 as stability in the transition zones, and
Penalty as the geological severity of the errors that remain. Under this protocol
GeoMERIT improves all three together, raising Weighted F1 from $0.7272$ to $0.7526$
($+0.0254$; $+3.5\%$), Boundary F1 from $0.4100$ to $0.4567$ ($+0.0467$; $+11.4\%$), and
Penalty from $-0.6289$ to $-0.5886$ ($+0.0403$; $+6.4\%$) (Table~\ref{tab:main},
Figure~\ref{fig:main}). That the three metrics move in the same direction is more informative than the magnitude of
any one of them. A model that simply favored the majority class could raise Weighted F1 while
degrading boundary and tail performance, and a model that over-corrected for rare classes could
raise those metrics while worsening its Penalty through geologically distant errors. A
simultaneous improvement on all three therefore indicates that GeoMERIT shifts the operating
point as a whole---overall quality, boundary stability, and geological cost---rather than
trading one against another; the rise in tail mean F1 from roughly $0.31$ at the single-model
baseline to $0.5061$ further confirms that the gains are not obtained at the expense of the
rarest classes.

\subsection{Where the Gains Come From}
\label{sec:rq2}
A simultaneous improvement on three axes raises the question of whether GeoMERIT simply
benefits from a stronger base classifier. To determine where the improvement originates, we
conduct three analyses: we locate GeoMERIT on the published RFE ablation ladder, verify that
the gain persists across different tree backbones, and isolate the decision layer within an
otherwise identical pipeline. On the ladder of~\cite{chen2026rfe} (Table~\ref{tab:ladder}),
quality-aware weighting and feature engineering each close part of the gap from the $0.6635$
baseline to $0.7272$, and GeoMERIT's added layers---the three-backend geometric ensemble, the
geological hierarchy, the tail experts, and Bayes-risk decoding---close a further $+0.0254$
Weighted F1 and $+0.0403$ Penalty on top of them. Because those rungs were not re-run inside
our stack, we interpret this final increment as the cumulative effect of the four added layers
rather than a per-layer decomposition; the decision layer is isolated causally further down in
a same-stack ablation, while the representation and learning layers are stress-tested directly
by the per-layer experiments of Sections~\ref{sec:rq3}--\ref{sec:rq4}.

The most direct evidence comes from the decision layer, which can be switched on and off without
altering anything beneath it. Holding the representation, learning, and posterior layers

\subsection{Robustness on Feature-Deficient Wells}
\label{sec:rq3}
If the representation layer encodes missingness as useful information rather than merely
masking it, then prediction accuracy should remain stable on wells with incomplete curve
suites. We test this directly by stratifying the held-out wells by mean per-row missingness and
comparing feature-complete against feature-deficient subsets at several thresholds, annotating each bucket with its size in wells so that single-well buckets can be
read apart from populated ones (Figure~\ref{fig:robust}). Performance on missingness-heavy
wells degrades gracefully rather than collapsing. The $38$-well deficient bucket ($0.1/0.4$ split) attains Weighted F1
$0.7103$ and Boundary F1 $0.4408$---above the all-wells RFE+XGBoost boundary reference of
$0.4100$ despite operating only on incomplete wells. The $8$-well deficient bucket
($0.2/0.5$) attains Weighted F1 $0.7555$, exceeding the all-wells reference of $0.7272$
outright. The only collapse, the $0.3/0.6$ deficient bucket, is a single-well bucket of
$11{,}315$ rows---i.e., one difficult well rather than a systematic failure mode; had the
representation layer merely memorized the training wells, the deficient buckets would have
fallen below the reference already at the populated thresholds. This is the directly
testable consequence of the design of Section~\ref{sec:repr}---the missingness indicators,
curve-suite fingerprint, and within-well normalization carry the information that
survives when curves disappear---and it addresses the practical case that motivates the
paper, namely prediction on a newly drilled well whose tool string differs from those in the
training wells.

\subsection{Per-Class Diagnosis, Tail Behavior, and Well Tracks}
\label{sec:rq4}
Because Weighted F1 is dominated by Shale, it can conceal what is happening to the rare classes
and the bed boundaries that the learning and posterior layers target; we therefore decompose it
class by class. We report per-class out-of-fold precision,
recall, and F1 (Table~\ref{tab:perclass}) and read them through a three-level diagnosis

\subsection{Decision-Layer Sensitivity}
\label{sec:rq5}
For the cost-guided decision layer to be useful in practice, its operating point should lie in
a stable region rather than at a sharp optimum. To assess this, we sweep its two free parameters
from Section~\ref{sec:decision}---the logit-adjustment temperature $\tau$ and the Dolomite gate
$(\gamma_\text{dol},\theta_\text{dol})$---while the rest of the pipeline is held fixed
(Figure~\ref{fig:decision}).

The two panels answer one question each. The $\tau$ sweep is monotone and flat near the
adopted value---between $\tau=0.1$ and $\tau=0.3$, Weighted F1 varies by only $0.0045$ and

force Dolomite F1 higher slide down the frontier, losing geological cost---consistent with a
class whose log response overlaps its carbonate neighbors (Section~\ref{sec:failures}). That
both surfaces are monotone and geologically readable is itself a benefit of separating
decoding from learning. A retraining-based cost treatment would have to re-fit the model to
expose the same trade-off curves.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Joint Gains Establish About Robustness}
\label{sec:joint}
The main result is not any single number but a pattern. Under a protocol that forbids
within-well leakage, strengthening the representation, the weighting, the posterior structure,
and the decoding against the conditions named above improved \emph{overall quality, boundary
stability, and geological cost at the same time}. The three improvements are
traceable to different layers. The Weighted F1 gain arises chiefly from the
enriched representation and backbone diversity; the Boundary F1 gain (the largest relative
gain, $+11.4\%$, and the only one individually fold-significant) from window statistics,
gradients, and boundary up-weighting; and the Penalty gain---now isolated causally by the
same-stack ablation of Section~\ref{sec:rq2}---from the Bayes-risk decoder, which by
construction cannot improve anything except expected cost. This decomposition matches the
design intent of Table~\ref{tab:layers} and supports the paper's central claim. Lithology
prediction under complex well-log conditions becomes more robust when data quality,
long-tail structure, boundary uncertainty, and geological cost are optimized jointly rather
than appended as post-processing.

\subsection{Failure Modes Are Geologically Structured}
\label{sec:failures}
The classes GeoMERIT still misses are not scattered model noise; each has a specific geological
cause, and naming that cause points to a concrete remedy rather than a vague call for more data.

\textbf{Dolomite (F1 $=0.040$)} is the largest visible failure, and its cause is mineralogical
rather than statistical. Clean dolomite (PEF $\approx 3.1$, matrix density
$\approx 2.87$~g/cm$^3$) sits squarely inside the carbonate subspace already occupied by
limestone (PEF $\approx 5$, $\approx 2.71$~g/cm$^3$), chalk, and marl, and the gate sweep of
Section~\ref{sec:rq5} showed that overlap to be strong enough that forcing dolomite recall
actively worsens the penalty. The remedy is therefore not a larger classifier but conditional
structure---a carbonate-subspace expert that operates only within the super-class, retrieval
priors restricted to the same GROUP and FORMATION, or a dedicated dolomite-versus-limestone
discriminator trained only where the two rocks coexist.

\textbf{Basement (F1 $=0.000$)} fails completely, but for a reason that lies in the data rather
than the model. Its 141 rows are concentrated in a few wells where the key curves are
themselves heavily missing, so under GroupKFold several training folds carry no Basement
supervision at all---a set of training wells that never logged basement cannot teach a model to detect it.
The right response is to treat extremely rare, spatially concentrated classes as open-set or
anomaly segments rather than forcing them into a twelfth softmax output.

\textbf{Sandstone/Shale ($0.347$) and Chalk ($0.357$)} are weak despite ample support---
Sandstone/Shale alone has $180{,}820$ rows---so here the ceiling is set by the labels, not by
rarity. Sandstone/Shale is by definition a transitional rock between its two parents, and
Chalk's combination of high precision ($0.815$) with low recall ($0.228$) describes a model
that commits to chalk only when the evidence is overwhelming and otherwise defers to limestone.
These are label-resolution limits at log scale, and moving past them will take either revisited
labels or sequence-level decoding that reasons about bed packages instead of individual rows.

\subsection{Scope and Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}
Three limitations bound the claims. First, the cross-stack comparison in
Table~\ref{tab:ladder} splices published rungs with our extension, so the four-layer
increment is attributed cumulatively rather than per-layer in that comparison; the
same-stack decision-layer ablation of Section~\ref{sec:rq2} provides the only directly
causal contrast within GeoMERIT, and a full in-stack leave-one-out ablation across all four
layers is the natural next experiment. Second, the Weighted F1 and Penalty gains, while
consistent in pooled OOF and directionally consistent across folds, are not individually
significant at $\alpha=0.05$ even under the one-sided test (Table~\ref{tab:perfold}); we
have deliberately not claimed otherwise. Third, all conclusions are for one basin and one
penalty matrix; the decision layer would inherit any context-dependence of $A$ (e.g., depth-
or formation-conditional costs), which we view as an opportunity---Equation~(\ref{eq:bayes})
accepts any cost matrix without retraining.

\subsection{Why a Tree-Only Stack Remains the Right Substrate Here}
\label{sec:trees}
GeoMERIT instantiates, in a geological setting, the broader observation that tree ensembles
remain highly competitive on tabular
data~\cite{grinsztajn2022trees,shwartzziv2022tabular,mcelfresh2024benchmark}. GBDTs natively
route the sentinel-encoded missingness states, remain insensitive to the
four-orders-of-magnitude scale differences across curves, use axis-aligned decision surfaces
that mirror the cross-plot logic of petrophysical interpretation, and accept categorical
features such as \texttt{curve\_suite\_id} and FORMATION as first-class inputs. None of
this precludes deep follow-ups---well-log foundation models~\cite{qi2025wlfm} could supply
upstream representations that the same weighting, hierarchy, and decoding layers consume,
and agentic workflows~\cite{wang2026geodecider} could invoke GeoMERIT as a calibrated
tool---but in the missingness-heavy, long-tailed, strictly well-grouped regime studied here,
the tree substrate is a modeling choice matched to the data, not a concession to compute.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We presented GeoMERIT, a missingness-aware and penalty-guided robust lithology prediction
method for incomplete, heterogeneous, and long-tailed well-log data. Whereas conventional
lithology classifiers optimize a single accuracy figure on idealized data, GeoMERIT instead
targets stability under the conditions that real logs present. It encodes missingness,
anomalies, and curve-suite identity as explicit state rather than imputing them away, then
lifts rare classes and boundary samples into the training objective, restructures the posterior
through a tree ensemble, a geological hierarchy, and tail experts, and finally chooses each
label by minimizing geological risk under the official penalty matrix rather than by maximizing
posterior probability. On the full FORCE 2020 valid-label set under strict
10-fold GroupKFold validation by well---the protocol that simulates prediction on unseen
wells---GeoMERIT attains Weighted F1 $=0.7526$, Macro F1 $=0.5315$, Boundary F1 $=0.4567$,
Penalty $=-0.5886$, and tail mean F1 $=0.5061$, improving the published RFE+XGBoost
reference simultaneously by $+3.5\%$ (Weighted F1), $+11.4\%$ (Boundary F1, fold-significant
at $p=0.002$), and $+6.4\%$ (Penalty). A same-stack ablation isolates the decision layer
causally, adding $+0.0080$ Weighted F1, $+0.0277$ Macro F1, $+0.0144$ Boundary F1, and $+0.0324$
Tail F1 at essentially unchanged Penalty. The decision layer converts posterior quality
into rare-class and boundary performance without paying geological cost. The most
consequential finding is structural. The three gains correspond to three distinct facets of
robustness and are produced by different, separable layers of the design, which means the
framework's components can be adopted independently---and its decision layer retuned to any
cost matrix without retraining. Future work follows directly from the diagnosed failure
modes. Dolomite calls for carbonate-subspace experts and geology-conditioned retrieval priors;
Basement-like classes are better posed as an open-set problem than as a softmax output;
semi-Markov segment decoding would let the decision layer reason at bed scale; and well-log
foundation models could supply the upstream representations beneath the same robust decision
stack.
